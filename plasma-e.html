Plasma Experiments

4-1. The Big Picture of Nuclear Fusion Research

Here, we will introduce a general overview of plasma diagnostics. Before doing so, let us explain the specific role and perspective our laboratory adopts in the field of magnetically confined nuclear fusion plasma research. Figure 4-1 illustrates the overall workflow of fusion research. The ultimate goal of fusion research is to build a commercial nuclear fusion reactor. As a vital preliminary step, plasma confinement devices are constructed to perform intensive experiments. Based on the insights gained from these experiments, engineering design activities for future fusion reactors are carried out. Furthermore, if new challenges emerge during the design process, it becomes necessary to execute targeted experiments and theoretical modeling on currently operating devices to meet those design requirements. Although the insights from modern plasma experiments must be actively utilized in reactor design, the knowledge applied to reactor engineering needs to be firmly established and verified. In my personal opinion, a slight gap in perspective exists between commercial reactor design and the latest academic plasma experiments or theoretical research; establishing a tight feedback loop as shown in Figure 4-1 remains an ongoing challenge for the future. Nevertheless, both plasma experiments/theories and reactor design are progressing steadily. We will continue these parallel efforts toward the 2040s and ultimately build commercial fusion reactors based on the comprehensive knowledge accumulated by then.

Research on current plasma devices covers the topics outlined in Figure 4-2. Various laboratories in the Department of Advanced Energy Engineering Science at Kyushu University take charge of different research items shown in Figure 4-2. Among them, our laboratory focuses on the experimental research of "plasma confinement," indicated in red text.

The scientific community is broadly divided into experimental and theoretical researchers; approximately 80% of researchers are engaged in experimental work, while the remaining 20% focus on theoretical studies. Theoretical research is carried out for almost all the experimental categories shown in Figure 4-2. For instance, in our field of plasma confinement, we execute discharges, diagnose the plasma, and evaluate its confinement performance. We then analyze and organize the experimental data to investigate what physical mechanisms govern the confinement. Concurrently, we collaborate with theoretical researchers or perform numerical modeling ourselves to unravel the underlying "physical principles." However, managing collaborative research between experiment and theory can be highly challenging. Modern scientific research—not limited to nuclear fusion—is highly fragmented and specialized. Consequently, researchers often lack a deep understanding of fields outside their immediate expertise. To make the comparison between experiment and theory truly successful, one must look beyond their own specialty and develop a solid understanding of their counterpart's field. To use an analogy, it is somewhat similar to human relationships—especially romantic ones. Mutual understanding is absolutely essential for a smooth relationship. When I was young, I struggled immensely with romantic relationships because I couldn't understand my partner, and they couldn't understand me either.

4-2. Classification of Plasma Diagnostics

Advanced diagnostics are indispensable for experimental research. The physical parameters required to evaluate a plasma include its density, temperature, electrical potential, magnetic field, light emission, particle transport, rotational velocity, and more. When classified by their measurement methodology, these diagnostics fall into the categories shown in Figure 4-3. If these plasma parameters can be recorded across a vast number of spatial measuring points, we can reconstruct their spatial structures. Furthermore, if we can capture rapid changes over time, we can evaluate the fluctuations of these parameters. Measuring spatial structures is crucial for evaluating plasma confinement performance. On the other hand, measuring fluctuations is key for different scales: large-scale fluctuations (macroscopic electromagnetic instabilities) are vital for studying how to maintain a stable plasma, whereas small-scale fluctuations (microscopic turbulence, which includes both electrostatic and electromagnetic modes) are believed to cause the degradation of energy and particle confinement. Our laboratory focuses specifically on developing laser-based interferometry and microwave-based scattering diagnostics. For further details on various plasma diagnostic methods, please consult References [7] through [9].

4-3. Introduction to Plasma Discharges (H-mode Transition in the LHD)

Figure 4-4 illustrates an example of a typical plasma discharge waveform on the LHD. Let us utilize this figure to explain how to interpret diagnostic datasets. This discharge represents a prominent example of the "H-mode" (High-confinement mode), a regime where the plasma confinement performance spontaneously improves during the discharge. Figures 4-4(a) and (b) display the injected power of the neutral beam injection (NBI) systems used for heating. The solid lines indicate the raw power injected into the vacuum vessel, evaluated from the acceleration voltage and beam current of the injectors. Evaluating the actual power absorbed by the plasma is somewhat more complicated. While it can be calculated via numerical simulations, the computation requires significant time. For a simpler and faster evaluation, carbon targets are installed inside the vacuum vessel directly opposite the NBI injection ports on the other side of the plasma. By measuring the temperature rise of these carbon targets using infrared cameras, we can record the "shine-through" power that passes completely through the plasma without being absorbed. Subtracting this unabsorbed shine-through power from the total injected power allows us to accurately evaluate the net power absorbed by the plasma.

Figure 4-4(c) displays the $\beta$ (beta) value, which represents the ratio of plasma thermal energy (stored energy) to magnetic field energy, expressed as a percentage. The stored energy is the product of plasma temperature and density. Remarkably, this can be evaluated without directly measuring local density or temperature, simply by tracking the electrical current changes in magnetic loops wound around the vacuum vessel. This is possible because the generation of plasma induces diamagnetic currents that flow in a direction that opposes the external magnetic field.

In terms of commercial reactor engineering, the most expensive components are the superconducting magnetic coils. Increasing the magnetic field strength generally enhances confinement performance, but it drives up construction costs exponentially. Therefore, a commercial reactor must be able to confine high-energy plasma using the lowest possible magnetic field strength. It is widely estimated that a beta value of approximately 5% is required for a fusion reactor to be economically viable. However, raising the beta value amplifies electromagnetic instabilities, making it exceptionally difficult to maintain a stable plasma duration. Successfully maximizing the beta value while suppressing instabilities and maintaining excellent confinement stands as one of the central challenges in magnetic confinement fusion research.

Figure 4-4(d) shows the temporal evolution of the line-averaged electron density measured by a far-infrared laser interferometer. The detailed principles of interferometers will be discussed on the "Interferometry and Thomson Scattering" page. The electron density recorded by an interferometer is the line-integrated value accumulated along the line-of-sight path of the laser beam through the plasma. Consequently, even for the same peak density, a larger plasma size yields a higher recorded value. Since actual physics calculations require the density value itself, the line-integrated density is divided by the physical path length through the plasma to yield the "line-averaged electron density." This parameter is most frequently used as the primary monitor for plasma density during experiments.

On the other hand, Figure 4-4(f) displays the line-integrated densities recorded across 11 distinct channels of the interferometer array. Note that the vertical axis unit in Figure 4-4(d) is $\text{m}^{-3}$, whereas the unit in Figure 4-4(f) is $\text{m}^{-2}$ because integrating the density along the laser path through the plasma multiplies the volumetric density ($\text{m}^{-3}$) by the path length ($\text{m}$). Each colored line in Figure 4-4(f) represents a measurement along a different viewing chord. Crucially, the temporal behavior varies significantly depending on the spatial chord position. These multi-channel interferometer datasets allow us to mathematically reconstruct the local spatial structure of the electron density, as detailed on the "Interferometry and Thomson Scattering" page.

By the way, while the reactions inside future reactors and current experimental devices were illustrated in Figure 3-1 and Figure 3-2, you will notice that the actual densities of fuel ions ($\text{T}^+$, $\text{D}^+$, $\text{H}^+$) do not appear in Figure 4-4. It is the ions that undergo fusion reactions, not the electrons. However, directly measuring local ion densities ($\text{T}^+$, $\text{D}^+$, $\text{H}^+$) is technically extraordinarily challenging. In a pure $\text{H}^+$ plasma, we can assume the ion density equals the electron density due to the plasma's macroscopic quasi-neutrality. However, when $\text{T}^+$ and $\text{D}^+$ are mixed together, standard electron density diagnostics cannot distinguish between the two ion species. Furthermore, even in standard $\text{H}^+$ experiments, various impurity ions ($\text{He}^{2+}$, $\text{C}^{6+}$, and multiple charge states of $\text{Fe}$ or $\text{Cr}$ eroded from the walls) are present, making the precise evaluation of the true fuel hydrogen ion density a highly intricate task.

Figure 4-4(e) displays the emission intensity of neutral hydrogen atoms ($H_\alpha$ line radiation). Hydrogen gas is introduced into the vacuum vessel via fuel injection valves or released naturally from the chamber walls. Inside the plasma boundary, hydrogen molecules dissociate into hydrogen atoms, which are subsequently ionized into free electrons and hydrogen ions. Figure 4-4(e) captures the light emitted when these neutral hydrogen atoms enter the plasma boundary, collide with free electrons, and transition from an excited high-energy state to a lower energy state. This emission intensity serves as an excellent indicator of the neutral hydrogen atom density—essentially tracking the volume of fuel recycling entering the plasma.

Figure 4-4(h) displays the temporal variation of the electron density fluctuation amplitude. This was recorded using a two-dimensional phase contrast imaging (2D-PCI) system. The 2D-PCI is an advanced diagnostic method developed extensively by our laboratory, capable of capturing the microscopic turbulence of electron density. Detailed principles will be introduced on the "Research" page. This micro-turbulence is believed to be directly responsible for dictating electron particle confinement.

With this diagnostic framework in mind, let us re-examine the discharge waveforms in Figure 4-4.

Fig. 4-4 H-mode discharge waveforms on the LHD
(a) Neutral beam injection (NBI) power per injector unit, (b) Total total NBI heating power, (c) $\beta$ = (Stored energy) / Magnetic energy, (d) Line-averaged electron density, (e) Hydrogen atom light emission intensity ($H_\alpha$), (f) Line-integrated electron density across individual interferometer chords, (g) Core electron temperature $T_e(0)$ and core ion temperature $T_i(0)$, (h) Plasma electron density fluctuation amplitude. In (a) and (b), solid lines indicate the power injected into the vacuum vessel, while dotted lines represent the net power absorbed by the plasma [10].

The acronym NBI stands for Neutral Beam Injection, a system that accelerates hydrogen ions to high voltages, neutralizes them into fast hydrogen atoms, and shoots them into the plasma. The particles must be neutralized because charged ions would be violently deflected by the immense magnetic confinement fields, preventing them from entering the core. The labels #1 through #4 indicate individual injector units. As shown in Figure 4-4(a), at $3.3\,\text{seconds}$, NIFS units NBI #1, #2, and #3 fire simultaneously, injecting a total of $13\,\text{MW}$ of power. While NBI is primarily used for plasma heating, our research group discovered that it can also be leveraged to successfully initiate plasma ionization and breakdown on the LHD [11]. Traditionally, helical systems initiate plasma breakdown using microwave electron cyclotron resonance heating, which operates on a principle similar to a kitchen microwave. However, this resonance method requires exceptionally expensive high-power microwave oscillators finely tuned to match the specific magnetic field strength, strictly limiting the range of magnetic fields available for experiments. Discovering that NBI can reliably ignite the plasma breakdown significantly expanded the operational range of magnetic fields for LHD experiments. The injected high-energy hydrogen atoms undergo successive collisions with the background particles, transferring their energy to heat the plasma.

At $3.7\,\text{seconds}$, the power of NBI #2 (red line) drops by half and terminates completely at $4.15\,\text{seconds}$ due to an unexpected hardware interlock. Simultaneously at $3.7\,\text{seconds}$, NBI #4 (green line) begins firing. Unlike the other units, NBI #4 is intentionally pulsed to deliver $2\,\text{MW}$ of power for $0.08\,\text{seconds}$, followed by a $0.02\,\text{second}$ pause. This is not a hardware malfunction; it is driven by a diagnostic requirement. To measure the local ion temperature and plasma rotation velocity via a method called Charge Exchange Spectroscopy (CXS), we observe the light emission from the beam particles. To isolate the true beam signal by subtracting the background plasma light emission, NBI #4 is momentarily paused for $0.02\,\text{seconds}$ in a regular modulation cycle. As seen in Figure 4-4(g), local ion temperature measurements become available the moment NBI #4 begins firing.

At $4.3\,\text{seconds}$, NBI #1 (black line) terminates. This shutdown was intentional. Simultaneously, the hydrogen atom emission intensity ($H_\alpha$) drops abruptly as shown in Figure 4-4(e), indicating a sudden reduction in fuel gas entering the plasma boundary. Interestingly, the actual volume of hydrogen gas being supplied through the gas puff valves did not change at $4.3\,\text{seconds}$. This phenomenon occurs because hydrogen fuel molecules adsorb heavily onto the inner walls of the vacuum vessel. Because the plasma boundary interacts continuously with the vessel wall, hydrogen particles released from the wall act as a significant fuel source (recycling). The sudden drop in $H_\alpha$ at $4.3\,\text{seconds}$ indicates that particle release from the wall decreased abruptly, triggered by the changes in edge plasma parameters when NBI #1 was turned off. Crucially, despite this significant reduction in fuel supply, the electron density does not decrease; instead, it begins to rise. This clearly demonstrates that the particle confinement of the electrons has enhanced. However, we cannot yet conclude that the global confinement state has spontaneously improved, because a reduction in total heating power naturally enhances baseline confinement parameters. As a general rule in plasma physics, increasing the heating power degrades the global energy confinement time—a fundamental obstacle to reaching ignition conditions.

However, the plasma's response around $4.8\,\text{seconds}$ deserves close attention. Look closely at the vertical red line marking the $4.8\,\text{second}$ timestamp. Around $4.8\,\text{seconds}$, the gas volume supplied by the puff valves remains completely constant. The $H_\alpha$ emission in Figure 4-4(e) decreases further, meaning the volume of fuel particles recycling from the walls into the plasma has dropped. Yet, unlike the event at $4.3\,\text{seconds}$, the total NBI heating power remains completely unchanged at $4.8\,\text{seconds}$ as shown in Figures 4-4(a) and (b). Despite a constant heating power and a reduced fuel influx, the electron density in Figures 4-4(d) and (f) begins to rise sharply. An increasing density under a reduced fuel supply and a constant heating power provides definitive proof that the particle confinement has not just changed, but the global confinement state has spontaneously improved. A phenomenon where a plasma abruptly shifts into a completely different operational state is called a "transition," and this specific self-improving transition is known as the H-mode transition. This density rise does not continue indefinitely; at $5.1\,\text{seconds}$, the density peak terminates and begins to decay. Around $5.1\,\text{seconds}$, the heating power remains constant and the fuel influx slightly increases, yet the density drops, marking the termination of the improved confinement state. As the saying goes, all good things must come to an end.

Fig. 4-5 Detailed waveforms of the H-mode transition boundaries

Let us analyze the rapid changes around $4.8\,\text{seconds}$ in finer detail. Figure 4-5 displays the line-integrated electron density from the interferometer, the magnetic field fluctuations measured by magnetic probes, and the electron density fluctuations captured by the 2D-PCI system. In Figure 4-5(a), the red line indicates a viewing chord passing through the inner region of the plasma, while the blue line tracks a chord near the outer boundary. Up until $4.8\,\text{seconds}$, we can observe a distinct series of jagged, sawtooth-like oscillations. At approximately $4.77\,\text{seconds}$, these oscillations suddenly vanish, and the signals become quiet. At $4.8\,\text{seconds}$, the inner density (red line) rises sharply, while the outer edge density (blue line) decreases. As shown in Figure 4-4(d), the line-averaged density begins its steep upward turn at $4.8\,\text{seconds}$. This behavior in Figure 4-5(a) reveals that the spatial gradient of the electron density at the plasma boundary has become exceptionally steep following the $4.8\,\text{second}$ boundary.

Figure 4-5(b) displays the signal from coils installed inside the vacuum vessel that measure rapid variations in the local magnetic field. These sensors are called magnetic probes. The magnetic fluctuation signal decreases gradually after $4.77\,\text{seconds}$ and completely vanishes at $4.82\,\text{seconds}$. Because plasma behaves as a magnetohydrodynamic fluid, changes in its macroscopic structure induce local current variations, which alter the surrounding magnetic field. The complete disappearance of this magnetic probe signal reveals that the underlying cause of these macroscopic electromagnetic fluctuations has been suppressed, which may directly contribute to the confinement transition. The most fascinating observation is the behavior of the microscopic electron density fluctuations measured by the 2D-PCI system shown in Figure 4-5(c). While the time-compressed view in Figure 4-4(h) makes it look like the signal simply intensifies between $4.5$ and $4.8\,\text{seconds}$, the detailed view in Figure 4-5(c) reveals that the turbulence is not continuously intense; rather, it appears as intermittent bursts of large-amplitude fluctuations. The moment the sawtooth oscillations disappear in Figure 4-5(a), these intermittent large-amplitude turbulence bursts vanish simultaneously. Furthermore, at $4.795\,\text{seconds}$—the exact moment the electron density begins its rapid upward climb—the baseline turbulence amplitude drops even further. Unlike the magnetic probe signal in Figure 4-5(b), this microscopic density turbulence drops in perfect synchronization with the initiation of the density rise in Figure 4-5(a), strongly indicating that the suppression of micro-turbulence is directly and causally responsible for driving the confinement improvement.

In this way, many things happen in a plasma's duration, just like in human life. There is a period where your hard work does not bear fruit (corresponding to 3.3–4.3 s). Then, a catalyst triggers a shift (4.3–4.8 s), and suddenly, everything starts going perfectly smoothly (4.8–5.1 s). However, it does not last forever and eventually comes to an end (after 5.1 s). We can truly learn life lessons from a plasma, can't we?
