Ask Prof. Tanaka (FAQ)

Here, I answer various questions received from students. Please note that these are strictly my personal opinions and perspectives.

Q1. What kind of place is Toki City, where NIFS is located?

A1 It is located about 40 minutes from Nagoya City via the JR Chuo Line. Although the institute itself is in Toki City, many students and staff choose to live in the neighboring Tajimi City. Both Toki and Tajimi have been famous as traditional pottery towns since the Sengoku period. Because they craft beautiful ceramic vessels, the local culinary culture has thrived, and there are many excellent restaurants to enjoy. Recently, an Aeon Mall—reputedly one of the largest in the Tokai region—opened here, and the area is steadily developing as a convenient bedroom community for Nagoya.

Q2. What kind of place is Nagoya City?

A2 With a population of 2.2 million, it is a massive metropolis and the central hub of the Tokai region. When I ask students for their impressions, many answer that it is a beautiful, clean, and vibrant city, even larger in scale than Hakata. The aerospace and automotive industries are highly active in the Tokai area, making it very convenient for students interested in factory tours and corporate visits. From the institute, it takes about an hour and a half by car via local roads, or just 40 minutes by train, making it a very accessible distance for weekend leisure. The Nagoya area features numerous stimulating museums for researchers, including the Nagoya City Science Museum, the SCMAGLEV and Railway Park, and the Toyota Exhibition Museums. Beyond traditional culture, it is also a major center for subcultures; it hosts the permanent theater for the idol group SKE48 (named after the central district "SAKAE"), and the World Cosplay Summit is held there every August, including the 2023 event in Sakae.

Q3. Is it absolutely necessary to move from Fukuoka Prefecture to Gifu Prefecture?

A3 Yes. During the first half of the Master's program, you will obtain your course credits at the Kyushu University Chikushi Campus in Kasuga City, Fukuoka Prefecture. After that, you will relocate to Toki City, Gifu Prefecture, to focus entirely on your laboratory research. Therefore, you will need to move to either Toki City or Tajimi City. When relocating to Gifu, you can stay in the institute's lodge for about the first month, which gives you ample time to inspect and select your apartment. Mr. Sakai actually followed this exact process to find his current apartment.

Q4. Are there other students at the institute? I'm worried I might get lonely if I am all by myself.

A4 You don't need to worry at all. In addition to the students from the Kyushu University collaborative course, NIFS hosts about 30 graduate students from the Graduate University for Advanced Studies (SOKENDAI), the Nagoya University collaborative course, the University of Tokyo collaborative course, and other universities nationwide participating in joint research projects. The student rooms function exactly like those in any standard university laboratory, and everyone enjoys conducting research together. Furthermore, alongside the Kenji Tanaka Laboratory, Kyushu University also operates the Taiji Kato Laboratory at NIFS, allowing Kyushu University students to readily exchange information and support each other.

Q5. Can I work a part-time job during my studies?

A5 I do not recommend working part-time jobs. Please dedicate your time as a graduate student to focus entirely on your coursework and research. Every year, the top 30% of students at the Kyushu University IGSES receive a full or half repayment exemption for their interest-free student loans (scholarships). Our laboratory has an excellent track record of students securing these exemptions. Therefore, investing the time you would spend on a part-time job into your research will yield better scientific outcomes and significantly increase your chances of having your scholarship repayments waived, which is far more beneficial in the long run.

Q6. What are the career paths and employment prospects like?

A6 Thanks to the hard work and dedication of our students, almost everyone has successfully secured employment at their top-choice organizations.
Major employers of our graduates include: Nitori, Chubu Electric Power, NTT Docomo, Hitachi, Kyushu University, etc.

Q7. I am hesitant about whether to pursue a Doctoral degree. Can I find a good job after graduating with a Ph.D.?

A7 Once you become a Ph.D. student, you can receive research assistant stipends in addition to standard scholarships. Ph.D. candidates are treated more like independent professional researchers than mere students. Entering a Doctoral program requires a profound passion for scientific research. It is true that securing a permanent academic or research position immediately after graduation is highly competitive; many graduates begin their careers as postdoctoral researchers for a few years before obtaining tenure. However, many private corporations are also increasingly hiring Ph.D. holders with excellent, commensurate benefits, and Kyushu University's career support team provides robust assistance. To my knowledge, I have never heard of a single graduate from the Kyudai IGSES who was unable to find employment.

Q8. What subjects should I focus on studying during my undergraduate years?

A8 In addition to foundational mathematics and physics, please make sure to study electromagnetism thoroughly. Furthermore, since the vast majority of our reference materials, textbooks, and scientific papers are written in English, dedicated language study is vital. Developing English conversation skills will serve you well, as fusion research is highly internationalized, offering frequent opportunities to debate with researchers from around the globe. This builds invaluable professional networks for your future. Especially recently, international conferences and research meetings are actively conducted online via platforms like Zoom. In such environments, it is important to be bold and vocalize your ideas in English without being shy.

Q9. How can I effectively develop my English language skills?

A9 Standard university English classes are completely insufficient on their own; you must make proactive individual efforts. To cultivate reading comprehension, you simply need to read a large volume of scientific papers. To build conversation skills, you must actively accumulate speaking experience. My secret tip is to always keep a repository of interesting stories or topics ("neta") ready, even if they are unrelated to your research. Utilizing online one-on-one English conversation schools is also an excellent and convenient option nowadays.

Q10. I have heard about fusion venture companies recently. Will future fusion research be driven primarily by ventures?

A10 Strictly speaking, a company can only be called a "venture business" once it begins generating actual profits. Currently, there is no company in the world generating electricity and turning a profit from a fusion reactor. Therefore, these companies are technically "startups" rather than ventures. Investors supply capital based on the immense future potential of fusion energy. These fusion startups exist in the US, Europe, and Japan. They generally fall into two categories: one group aims to advance conventional tokamak or helical concepts more rapidly than government institutions, while the other group pursues entirely alternative, radical fusion concepts. Some US startups in the former group have raised over 200 billion yen, drawing prominent researchers away from government laboratories. On the other hand, the concepts of the latter group are so radical that they appear to face a mountain of unresolved scientific and technical hurdles.
I believe that government-funded national laboratories will continue to lead mainstream fusion research, but the breakthroughs and agile approaches of these startups provide healthy stimulation, ultimately accelerating the overall progress of fusion energy development.

Q11. Can we generate electrical energy from nuclear fusion right inside our laboratory?

A11 Unfortunately, we cannot generate actual electricity yet. Currently, we are focused on conducting fundamental physics research necessary to build future commercial fusion reactors. As shown in Figure 4-2 on the "Plasma Experiments" page, nuclear fusion research encompasses a vast array of specialized fields. Among them, our laboratory tackles plasma confinement, which is widely considered the most critical scientific challenge. We aim for our research outcomes to be directly utilized in the construction of future fusion reactors.

Q12. The ultimate goal of commercial fusion power lies so far in the future. Doesn't it feel overwhelming?

A12 We will likely see the first electricity-generating fusion reactor operational in about 30 years. If the agile initiatives of startups succeed, it might be even sooner. Thirty years is really not that long of a time. I can still vividly recall my numerous blunders as a student 30 years ago (especially in my personal life), and sometimes I wake up from nightmares about them! Furthermore, setting aside the ultimate macroeconomic energy goal, the physics of fusion plasma is deeply fascinating in its own right. For example, simply seeing a complex laser diagnostic system operate successfully brings immense joy. Analyzing turbulence data and discovering a completely new physical feature is exhilarating. While the initial motivation to start research is driven by a grand dream, a mission, or a sense of purpose, what sustains your daily research is the immediate, captivating interest right in front of you. Our laboratory is full of these rewarding moments.

Q13. Is there a big difference between students today and when you were a student?

A13 It is said that ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs contained inscriptions complaining, "Young people these days are no good!" Older generations always tend to criticize the youth, but I completely disagree with that view. Many modern students possess far more structured, mature thoughts than we did when we were young. The biggest difference between 30 years ago and today is the sheer volume of information available. With the internet, vast amounts of information flood our daily lives. While looking things up online is perfectly fine, I occasionally feel that modern students spend slightly less time sitting down to contemplate complex mathematical or physical concepts deeply. I clearly remember that 30 years ago, without smartphones or the internet, communication was a massive hurdle. If you wanted to contact a girl, you had to call her landline at home. Sometimes, her father would answer the phone. When sternly asked, "What is your relationship with my daughter?", I would panic and almost blurt out, "I haven't done anything to her yet!"

Q14. Are researchers popular with women?

A14 Unfortunately, simply being a researcher does not make you popular with women at all.
However, you can certainly use your unique research as an engaging topic of conversation. I had an experience like this a very long time ago: I was on a drive with a woman, and the conversation suddenly lapsed into a quiet silence, perhaps while waiting at a red light. It was a Sunday evening, and a strong, dramatic sunset cast its golden light straight through the windshield. I pointed toward the setting sun and told her:
"I am going to build a sun."
"What?!"
"My job is to realize the same nuclear fusion reactions occurring inside the sun right here on Earth. You see, hydrogen has three isotopes, and we can trigger the same fusion reaction between deuterium and tritium. To make it happen, we have to confine them at an incredibly high energy state. The sun achieves this using its immense gravity, but since we can't do that on Earth, we use powerful superconducting magnets instead..."
Since I was holding the steering wheel and focusing on driving, I couldn't see her expression clearly, but I am absolutely certain she was deeply moved by my story. Unfortunately, I never had the opportunity to see that woman again, but we shared a wonderfully lively and memorable evening talking about nuclear fusion.
