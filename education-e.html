Graduate Education

The Kenji Tanaka Laboratory belongs to the Department of Advanced Energy Engineering Science, Interdisciplinary Graduate School of Engineering Sciences (IGSES), Kyushu University. This laboratory operates as a "collaborative course" in partnership with the National Institute for Fusion Science (NIFS) and is located within the NIFS facility in Toki City, Gifu Prefecture. Professor Kenji Tanaka, who leads the course, belongs to NIFS and concurrently holds a professorship at Kyushu University. A collaborative course is a system where Kyushu University and other leading national research institutions jointly operate a university laboratory. This enables graduate students to receive high-quality university education while actively participating in cutting-edge scientific research at a national institute.

Master's students complete their course credits at the Kyushu University Chikushi Campus in Fukuoka during the first half of their first year (M1), and then move to NIFS in Gifu to conduct full-scale research using the Large Helical Device (LHD), one of the world's premier magnetic confinement plasma experimental devices.

For those considering the Doctoral program, it is essential to establish a meticulous 5-year research plan to yield significant academic achievements. While entering a Ph.D. program brings questions regarding financial stability and future career paths, what matters most is your genuine passion for physics and a strong determination to overcome the scientific challenges you will encounter. If you are hesitant about pursuing a Ph.D., we highly encourage you to consult with us individually.

For students planning to enter the industry after completing their Master's degree, our goal is to help you gain rigorous experience in experimental research and develop professional skills to summarize and present your findings effectively. Even if you transition into a completely different field after graduation, the fundamental workflow—preparing a project, delivering solid results, and reporting them clearly—remains identical across all professional careers. Though two years is a short period, we want you to accumulate as many diverse experiences as possible.

To provide a clearer and more convincing picture of daily student life from a firsthand perspective, below is a message from our alumnus, Shota Takeshida (written when he was a student here).

Below, I will provide a brief overview based on my own personal judgment and bias!!!!!

1. Regarding the Location of the Institute

The institute is located on the Toki City side of the border between Tajimi City and Toki City in Gifu Prefecture.

Toki City has been famous as a traditional pottery town since the Sengoku period. Tajimi City is a cozy town with a population of around 100,000, but since it functions as a bedroom community for Nagoya, it has absolutely everything you need for daily life (convenience stores, supermarkets, general stores, hospitals, hair salons, electronics stores, Nitori, and various izakayas).

As for housing, I highly recommend living around Tajimi Station. You can rent a high-quality apartment starting from around 30,000 yen per month, which offers excellent value for money.

For nightlife, weekend entertainment, or shopping, heading to Nagoya is the best option!!!! It takes only 40 minutes from Tajimi Station via the JR Chuo Line, and the last train returning from Nagoya to Tajimi leaves at 24:05.

2. Regarding Student Life

The institute hosts not only students from Kyushu University but also from Nagoya University, the Graduate University for Advanced Studies (SOKENDAI), and international students from all over the world. Interacting with peers regardless of nationality on a daily basis naturally helps improve your language and communication skills. Furthermore, because the total number of students within the entire institute is relatively small, the student community is exceptionally close-knit. Students frequently enjoy going out for dinner together, planning weekend trips, or playing sports. In terms of research, since people from various scientific backgrounds gather here, you can easily ask questions to specialists in almost any field, and they will always give you kind and thorough answers.

3. Regarding Job Hunting (Career Paths)

I know many prospective students place a heavy emphasis on this aspect. Navigating job hunting successfully is one of the most critical milestones during the two years of a Master's program. Before enrolling, I was incredibly anxious about this myself. I kept worrying: "Will I have enough time for job hunting?", "Since I'm far away from the main Kyushu University campus, will I miss out on critical career information?", or "Can I actually secure a good job?"

To be completely honest, the answer is: "The lab is highly supportive of job hunting, so it entirely depends on your own effort." Personally, I never faced any disadvantages in my job search due to being at this research institute. In fact, it offered nothing but advantages, and I received wonderful job offers from multiple major corporations. If you work hard here day by day, excellent career results will naturally follow.

4. Informal Q&A

Q. What kind of professor is Prof. Tanaka?
A. He is a professor full of passion who truly loves his work and deeply cares about his students.

Q. Is there a strict "core time" (mandatory lab hours)?
A. It depends on the experimental season, but he fundamentally respects and values students' autonomy.

Q. How am I treated academically after graduation?
A. You will receive a formal Master's degree from the Graduate School of Kyushu University, exactly the same as students on the main campus.

5. Closing Remarks

To prevent any unexpected surprises after you join, I want to be completely straightforward. Because this is a national research institution, life here is not just a walk in the park. Professor Tanaka genuinely cares about his students, but he is also incredibly strict and stoic when it comes to the quality of research. To be honest, there were days when I jokingly thought, "Am I going to survive this intense workflow?!" (laughs). However, by pushing through and keeping up with the high-level research environment at this national institute, I successfully navigated my job search and even received academic awards for my research.

Your current academic grades do not matter. If you have vitality, grit, and determination, you can accomplish anything. My own exam scores and grades during my undergraduate years were too terrible to look at! The rest is entirely up to your actions. As it is often said, changing your environment is the most critical step for personal growth. Let’s make the most of your final two years of student life!!!

As the proverb goes, "Seeing is believing." If you have even a slight interest, please come visit the institute. We will guide you around with more enthusiasm than you expect!!! (The junior students will!)

In addition, Hikaru Sakai, a Ph.D. student, has provided further advice reflecting the latest environment in our laboratory.

Below, I will explain the unique aspects of the collaborative course in more detail.

1. Strengths of the Collaborative Course

When visualizing a typical graduate school laboratory, you might imagine a student room located within a standard university building on campus, where students from the same group sit together, chatting and working on their research. If you choose Kyushu University, you probably envision a student life in Fukuoka. Given that, is there a clear advantage to choosing a collaborative course that requires moving to a different prefecture right in the middle of Honshu? Let me answer this question.

First and most obviously, it is the sheer scale and quality of the research facilities. In addition to NIFS's world-class Large Helical Device (LHD), the institute is equipped with an array of state-of-the-art diagnostic and experimental systems, enabling large-scale research projects that are simply impossible in a standard university laboratory.

Second is the depth of academic support. Since you hold affiliations with both Kyushu University and NIFS, you will receive a significantly larger volume of announcements regarding research seminars, workshops, and academic meetings, which serves as an excellent catalyst for broadening your scientific horizons. Furthermore, having the opportunity to attend advanced lectures hosted by SOKENDAI is a major benefit.

While this might sound like it only benefits highly ambitious students or those pursuing a Ph.D., a collaborative course functions as a major advantage even if you graduate after your Master's degree. As Mr. Takeshida mentioned, it is exceptionally strong for job hunting. Major heavy-industry corporations are well-aware of NIFS's prestige. Even outside those industries, the fact that you spent two years conducting research at a national institute under the Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology (MEXT) gives you a unique edge and demonstrates strong proactiveness compared to typical applicants. It becomes a powerful weapon if utilized correctly.

On a more personal note, establishing a base in a completely new region allows you to travel and explore various scenic areas located in the center of Japan. Since it is your final stretch as a student, stepping into a completely fresh environment can be an incredibly rewarding adventure.

2. Regarding Financial Support

Many graduate students rely on student loans or scholarships, and Japan offers a prominent "Repayment Exemption System" for category-1 loans. Simply put, if you achieve excellent research results and receive a recommendation from your department, half or all of your loan repayment will be waived. Since roughly 30% of all students nationwide receive this exemption, the barrier is by no means impossibly high. In the Tanaka Lab, we have an outstanding track record of students securing these exemptions, so you can receive proper guidance on how to fulfill the requirements. Even a half-exemption amounts to a very substantial financial sum, making it highly worth aiming for.

Additionally, since your tuition fees are paid to Kyushu University, you can apply for Kyushu University's tuition waiver program. There are three tiers of waivers: 1/4, 1/2, and full exemption. Including the 1/4 waiver, a significant number of students among my acquaintances receive some form of financial relief. Numerous scholarships from private foundations are also accessible.

For those intending to pursue a Ph.D., the financial support becomes even more robust. By being selected for various fellowship programs, you can receive a monthly stipend of approximately 200,000 yen for research assistance. The most prestigious program is the JSPS Research Fellowship (DC1/2), which our alumnus Mr. Kinoshita received. However, even if you are selected as a NIFS Research Fellow like myself, you can receive an equivalent level of financial support. Furthermore, Kyushu University offers special awards that can grant full tuition exemptions for Ph.D. students. Recently, financial backing for doctoral candidates has expanded significantly, allowing students to dedicate themselves entirely to research without financial worry.

As you can see, robust financial support systems are well-established for both Master's and Doctoral courses, which I encourage you to consider as a reassuring factor when planning your academic path.

3. Final Words

Keywords like "grit" and "vitality" mentioned by Mr. Takeshida might sound intimidating to modern students, as they are often associated with outdated or overly demanding work cultures. However, times have changed. Today, our lab strongly emphasizes helping each individual student find a research style that fits them best as they work toward the ultimate goal of graduation. Naturally, there are times when we work late hours or experience busy periods right before academic conferences or manuscript deadlines, but a grueling, exhausting daily routine is never forced upon you. Professor Tanaka genuinely listens to students' opinions. By maintaining open communication with him, you can effectively balance your research with job hunting, conferences, and paper writing, ensuring a comfortable and highly productive graduate school life.

It is natural to have uncertainties, so if you hold even a slight interest in the Tanaka Laboratory, please take the opportunity to visit us at NIFS. I would be more than happy to explain things further and share my experiences with you directly!
