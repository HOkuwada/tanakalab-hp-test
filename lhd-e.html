Large Helical Device (LHD)

3-1. History of Nuclear Fusion Research

Before introducing the Large Helical Device (LHD) at the National Institute for Fusion Science (NIFS), let us briefly look at the history of nuclear fusion research. As shown in Table 1-1 on the "What is Nuclear Fusion" page, compared to the rapid development of nuclear fission for power generation, technology using nuclear fusion has been under research and development for a very long time, yet it has not yet reached commercial electricity generation. Let's take a broad overview of this history. Table 3-1 summarizes the history of nuclear fusion R&D.

Table 3-1

1950s

  • Proposals for magnetically confined fusion systems are introduced.
  • 1958: The 2nd UN International Conference on the Peaceful Uses of Atomic Energy (Geneva). Fusion research is declassified globally.

1960s

  • 1961: The 1st IAEA Fusion Energy Conference (Salzburg). [Fusion research enters the "Age of Purgatory."] Many confinement concepts are tested.
  • The Tokamak concept is proposed in the USSR. In 1968, breakthrough results are achieved, but western nations are initially skeptical. A team from the Culham Laboratory (UK) brings a laser Thomson scattering diagnostic to the Kurchatov Institute and confirms the high electron temperature (1 keV).

1970s

  • The Tokamak becomes the mainstream concept worldwide.

1980s

  • Large-scale tokamaks (JET in the EU, JT-60 in Japan, TFTR in the US) are constructed.
  • In Japan, experiments on the medium-scale helical device, Heliotron E (Kyoto University), begin.

1990s

  • Design of the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER) begins through global collaboration.
  • Construction of the Large Helical Device (LHD) begins at NIFS (Experiments started in 1998).

2000s and Beyond

  • Construction of ITER begins. Helical/stellarator systems make steady progress. Numerical simulation techniques advance rapidly, enabling detailed comparisons between experiment and theory.
  • 2016: Experiments begin on the large-scale superconducting stellarator, Wendelstein 7-X, in Germany.
  • 2017: High-performance plasma experiments using deuterium begin on the LHD.
  • 2024: Superconducting tokamak JT-60SA (QST, Japan) begins operation.
  • 2025: ITER scheduled to start operation.

Interestingly, modern nuclear fusion research was triggered by a strange newspaper announcement from Argentina. In 1951, Juan Perón, then President of Argentina, proclaimed that his country had developed a new type of atomic energy that did not require uranium. This announcement sent shockwaves through the United States and the Soviet Union, both of which were actively developing thermonuclear weapons (hydrogen bombs). Lyman Spitzer, an American astrophysicist, recognized that the announcement was a hoax, but the news inspired him to conceive the idea of using magnetic fields to confine high-temperature plasma [6]. Consequently, research into magnetic confinement fusion began in the early 1950s. For the first decade, progress was slow. In 1961, at the concluding speech of the 1st IAEA Conference on Plasma Physics and Controlled Nuclear Fusion Research in Salzburg, Lev Artsimovich, the leader of Soviet fusion research, remarked that fusion research was currently in a "state of purgatory." Purgatory refers to a period of intense suffering; in Catholic theology, it is a place where souls undergo temporary suffering to expiate their sins before entering heaven. This meant that despite immense efforts, research had not yet shown clear progress.

The major turning point came in 1968 with an experiment in the Soviet Union. A new concept called the "Tokamak" was developed in the USSR, rumored to be conceived by the Nobel Peace Prize laureate Dr. Andrei Sakharov. The word "tokamak" is a Russian acronym for "toroidal chamber with magnetic coils." While previous magnetic confinement devices relied entirely on external electromagnets, the tokamak forms a doughnut-shaped plasma and drives a massive electrical current directly through the plasma along the torus axis. This plasma current generates its own magnetic field to help confine the plasma. At that time, one of the primary obstacles to raising plasma temperature was the presence of impurities inside the vacuum vessel. Although fusion experiments are conducted using hydrogen isotope plasmas, metal atoms (primarily iron) from the vacuum wall or oxygen from water vapor would enter the plasma as "impurities," causing severe radiation loss and cooling the plasma. The plasma current driven in a tokamak not only creates the necessary magnetic field but also heats the plasma via ohmic heating, just like an electric toaster wire getting hot. When impurities entered the plasma, the plasma resistance increased, which paradoxically enhanced the ohmic heating power. This positive feedback loop led to a dramatic rise in plasma temperature. Additionally, the manufacturing precision of electromagnets at that time was insufficient to create a stable confinement field solely from external coils. Because this occurred during the height of the Cold War, exchange between Soviet and Western researchers was heavily restricted. However, the Culham Laboratory in the UK proposed to verify the Soviet tokamak results. They brought their newly developed ruby laser Thomson scattering diagnostic to Moscow and measured the electron temperature directly, proving it had indeed reached 10 million degrees Celsius (1 keV). Based on this historic verification, the tokamak became the dominant mainstream concept worldwide. In contrast, Japan has uniquely pursued a dual-track approach, investigating both the tokamak and helical configurations in parallel.

3-2. Principles of High-Temperature Plasma Confinement

Let us briefly review the nuclear fusion reaction. As shown in Figure 2-1(a), the fusion reaction between a deuterium ion and a tritium ion generates a helium ion (alpha particle) and a neutron. The key physical property here is that charged particles, such as ions and electrons, naturally spiral around magnetic field lines. We exploit this property to confine the deuterium and tritium fuel ions, as well as the high-energy helium ions produced by the fusion reaction. As shown in Figure 2-6, the fusion cross-section (reaction probability) peaks at a temperature of approximately 10 keV (100 million degrees Celsius). In a fusion reactor, the plasma is initially raised to high temperatures using external heating systems, such as microwave electron cyclotron resonance heating or high-energy neutral particle beams (which will be deuterium beams in future reactors). Once the fusion reactions ignite, high-energy helium ions carrying 3.52 MeV are generated, which automatically provide additional heating to the core plasma. When this self-heating by helium ions becomes dominant and sustains the reaction, external heating systems can be turned off. This state is known as the "self-ignition condition," which requires confining a plasma with a density of $10^{20}\,\text{m}^{-3}$ ($100\,\text{trillion particles/cm}^3$) and a temperature above 10 keV (approx. 100 million degrees Celsius) for several seconds. To describe the state of a plasma, we use density and temperature. A density of $10^{20}\,\text{m}^{-3}$ might be hard to visualize. Density represents the number of particles per unit volume, and in fusion, the vital factor is the density of fuel hydrogen ions. Since directly measuring ion density is technically challenging, we evaluate it using electron density, taking advantage of the plasma's macroscopic electrical neutrality. In a pure hydrogen plasma, since the charge of a hydrogen ion is 1, the electron density is exactly equal to the ion density. However, if impurities are present, the fuel ion density becomes lower than the electron density. A density of $10^{20}\,\text{m}^{-3}$ has been achieved in many modern devices, including the LHD, and represents a high-density regime for current plasma physics. However, the density of the air we breathe at 0°C and 1 atm is $2.7 \times 10^{25}\,\text{m}^{-3}$ (Loschmidt's number), which is vastly denser than a fusion plasma. Because plasma must be generated under ultra-high vacuum conditions, its density is naturally orders of magnitude lower than that of the atmosphere. Temperature measurements also require clarification. Expressing plasma temperature in Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K) can be awkward, so we use electron volts (eV). One eV is the energy gained by an electron accelerated through an electrical potential difference of 1 volt, corresponding to approximately 11,600 K. Furthermore, the particles in a gas or plasma do not all share a single, uniform velocity; they possess a wide range of energies whose statistical average defines the temperature. When a system reaches thermal equilibrium, this statistical distribution takes the form of a normal distribution known as a Maxwellian distribution, and the temperature is defined by the width of this distribution. In Figure 3-1, the ions that have reached thermal equilibrium and form a Maxwellian distribution are called "bulk ions" ("bulk" meaning the vast majority). On the other hand, the helium ions produced immediately after a fusion reaction have not yet reached thermal equilibrium and are called "fast ions" (or energetic ions). These fast ions transfer their energy to the bulk ions via collisions, eventually slowing down to the bulk temperature and joining the Maxwellian distribution. In a commercial reactor, once these helium ions finish heating the plasma and slow down, they are no longer useful for fusion and dilute the deuterium-tritium fuel; thus, they are called "helium ash." In contrast, current experimental devices utilize light hydrogen or deuterium for experiments due to safety regulations and to prevent severe activation of components; tritium is not introduced. Therefore, as shown in Figure 3-2, no helium ions are produced via DT reactions. Instead, high-energy fast ions are artificially injected using neutral beam injection systems. These externally injected fast ions mimic the behavior of fusion-produced alpha particles, allowing us to study the confinement and transport physics of energetic particles.

Another crucial concept is "confinement." A very common misunderstanding is confusing plasma confinement time with plasma duration. The Large Helical Device has achieved steady-state discharges lasting roughly one hour, meaning the plasma was maintained for an hour. However, the energy confinement time during this discharge is only about 0.1 seconds. The LHD successfully maintained the plasma for an hour by continuously injecting high-power microwaves (77 GHz, 154 GHz) for electron heating and high-frequency radio waves (40–80 MHz) for ion heating. Yet, the moment these heating systems are turned off, the plasma energy decays within about 0.1 seconds. To use an analogy of an electric kettle: the duration is how long the kettle is plugged into the wall to keep the water hot, while the confinement time is how long the water stays hot after you unplug it from the wall. Achieving a confinement time of several seconds, as required by the self-ignition condition, is an extraordinarily difficult target for magnetic confinement devices.

Do you know someone around you who always has a romantic partner, but the person changes constantly? That is a steady-state duration. They fall in love instantly but cool down just as fast. This means their "confinement of affection" is poor. In contrast, falling in love with a single partner and staying in love for a long time represents excellent confinement. By the way, my own personal confinement time for affection is exceptionally long!!

With this understanding, try to visualize what it takes to simultaneously satisfy the three conditions of self-ignition: a density of $10^{20}\,\text{m}^{-3}$, a temperature above 10 keV (100 million degrees), and a confinement time of several seconds. Achieving all three values at once is a monumental challenge.

Next, let us briefly explain the principles of magnetic plasma confinement using a video simulation. Please click the link to view the video for Figure 3-3. You can see a positively charged ion spiraling around a green magnetic field line. In reality, both ions and electrons spiral around the fields, though electrons rotate in the opposite direction to ions. If we use straight magnetic field lines, the plasma simply escapes from the open ends. To eliminate these ends, the field lines are bent into a closed toroidal loop (a doughnut shape). Unfortunately, simply bending the field lines into a simple ring is not enough to confine the plasma. As you can see in the video, the ions quickly drift outward and escape. This occurs because bending the magnetic field into a ring naturally makes the magnetic field strength weaker on the outer side of the torus. This spatial gradient and the curvature of the field lines force ions and electrons to drift vertically in opposite directions. This charge separation sets up a strong vertical electric field, and the resulting $\vec{E} \times \vec{B}$ Lorentz force violently ejects both electrons and ions outward. However, if we twist the magnetic field lines as they orbit the torus, the positions of the vertically separating positive and negative particles are continuously swapped, canceling out the vertical electric field. As a result, excellent plasma confinement can be achieved.

Fig. 3-3 Principle of plasma confinement using magnetic fields (Video)

Whether in a tokamak or a helical device, twisting the toroidal magnetic field lines is the fundamental requirement. A tokamak achieves this twist by driving a massive electrical current directly through the plasma, whereas a helical device utilizes uniquely twisted external electromagnets (helical coils) to generate the twisted field structurally without requiring a plasma current. Both approaches have clear advantages and disadvantages. As mentioned in Section 3-1, the tokamak has achieved stellar performance milestones, but it requires continuously driving and sustaining the plasma current. Maintaining this current over long periods is a major research challenge. Furthermore, if a large electromagnetic instability develops in the plasma, the plasma current can terminate abruptly in an event called a "disruption." This induces tremendous electromagnetic forces on the vacuum vessel, risking severe mechanical damage to the device. In contrast, a helical system can maintain its confinement field indefinitely as long as electricity is supplied to the external coils, completely avoiding the risk of destructive current disruptions. However, creating a twisted, ring-like magnetic field structurally requires exceptionally complex 3D coil shapes, demanding highly sophisticated, high-precision manufacturing technologies. Furthermore, the energy confinement performance of helical systems historically lags slightly behind that of tokamaks. This is because the complex 3D magnetic structure inherent to helical systems can enhance particle orbit losses, leading to a degradation of confinement performance.

Currently, the tokamak stands as the dominant mainstream concept in global fusion research; approximately 80% of fusion scientists worldwide work on tokamaks. In Japan, however, helical research has been pursued continuously since the 1950s, and roughly half of the nation's fusion researchers investigate helical systems. Which configuration will ultimately form the basis of the first commercial fusion power plant remains a subject of active debate among scientists. Investigating both paths in parallel expands our engineering options for future reactor designs, broadens the foundation of plasma physics, and ultimately accelerates the global progress of fusion energy development.

3-3. The Large Helical Device

Helical device research in Japan was pioneered in the 1950s by Professor Koji Uo at Kyoto University. Named after Helios, the ancient Greek god of the sun, the "Heliotron" series began with Heliotron A, culminating in 1980 with the operation of Heliotron E, which was the largest helical device in the world at that time. I (Tanaka) conducted experiments on Heliotron E during my doctoral studies as a visiting researcher. Professor Atsuo Iiyoshi (who succeeded Prof. Uo, became the first Director-General of NIFS, and is currently Chancellor of Chubu University) and Professor Osamu Motojima (who became the third Director-General of NIFS and later Director-General of the ITER Organization) led the group to propose a massive scaling-up of the Heliotron concept. This led to the construction of the Large Helical Device (LHD) in Toki City, Gifu Prefecture. At that time, intense debates occurred among fusion researchers in Japanese universities regarding the creation of a major national device and which confinement concept should be selected. Ultimately, the choices narrowed down to a large tokamak or a large heliotron. Following fierce academic discussions, the community decided to construct the heliotron-type device.

Figure 3-4 shows an external photograph of the LHD. The LHD core sits at the center, surrounded by numerous heating systems (neutral beam injectors, transmission waveguides for microwave heating), high-vacuum pumps, and a vast array of diagnostic instruments. The physical scale of the LHD is approximately equal to that of the Great Buddha of Nara. Since a photograph can be difficult to interpret due to the dense surrounding equipment, Figure 3-5 provides a simplified illustration of the LHD core structure. The twisted, semi-transparent red torus represents the high-temperature plasma. It closely resembles the helical doughnut shown in Figure 2-8(b) on the "What is Nuclear Fusion" page. Surrounding the plasma volume are the blue helical coils, which are made entirely of superconducting materials. Most of the magnetic coils on the LHD are superconducting, except for specific magnetic field correction coils which utilize standard copper conductors. The vacuum vessel features numerous vertical and horizontal cylinders extending outward; these are called "ports." These ports are used to inject heating beams and high-power microwaves, introduce diagnostic laser beams, and observe light emission from the core plasma.

During the planning phase of the LHD, intense debates occurred regarding whether the magnetic coils should be conventional copper coils (copper conductors cooled by water) or superconducting coils cooled by liquid helium to achieve zero electrical resistance. Conventional coils would have significantly lowered the initial construction costs. Superconducting coils not only increased costs but also pushed the boundaries of the manufacturing technologies available at that time, meaning success was not guaranteed. Ultimately, the team decided to utilize superconducting coils because continuous, steady-state operation is an absolute requirement for a commercial fusion reactor, and developing this industrial-scale technology was a primary mission of the national institute. In large-scale scientific projects, leaders must occasionally make high-stakes decisions. While these decisions are based on past achievements and future potential, they are also heavily influenced by the personalities of the leaders. I had the privilege of knowing both Professor Iiyoshi and Professor Motojima personally; Professor Iiyoshi was gentle yet highly competitive and aggressive in pushing boundaries, while Professor Motojima was extraordinarily meticulous and cautious in execution. Looking back, the decision to build a fully superconducting device was absolutely correct. Utilizing superconducting coils allows us to run massive electrical currents through the coils continuously without generating heat. LHD experimental campaigns typically run for about five months every year, from autumn through winter, intentionally avoiding the peak electricity demand of summer. Throughout the experimental campaign, the coils are kept continuously in a superconducting state using liquid helium. On a typical experimental day, the coil currents are ramped up around 9:00 AM and ramped down before 7:00 PM. While steady-state long-pulse experiments can sustain plasma for up to an hour, standard high-performance experiments targeting maximum temperatures and densities operate in a short-pulse mode, producing plasma for 5 to 10 seconds every 3 minutes. This 3-minute interval is the time required for diagnostic systems to collect the massive amounts of data and write them to the computer hard drives. In contrast, devices utilizing conventional copper coils experience intense Joule heating from the coil resistance during a shot; consequently, researchers must wait a long time for the coils to cool down before firing the next shot. Even on a medium-scale device like Heliotron E, the cooling period required about 10 minutes, and large-scale conventional devices require even longer. By utilizing superconducting coils, the LHD could execute short-pulse experiments at a high frequency, enabling us to accumulate an enormous volume of experimental data in a relatively short timeframe.
