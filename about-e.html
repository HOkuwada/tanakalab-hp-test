Kenji Tanaka Laboratory is a part of the Major in Plasma and Quantum Engineering, Department of Advanced Energy Engineering Science, Interdisciplinary Graduate School of Engineering Sciences, Kyushu University. This laboratory is a collaborative course operated in cooperation with the National Institute for Fusion Science (NIFS) of the National Institutes of Natural Sciences, and is located within the NIFS facility in Toki City, Gifu Prefecture. Professor Kenji Tanaka, who leads the laboratory, belongs to NIFS and concurrently serves as a professor at Kyushu University. A collaborative course is a system in which Kyushu University and other domestic research institutions jointly operate a university laboratory. This allows graduate students to receive high-quality education from their home university while actively participating in cutting-edge research. Students will obtain a Master's or Doctoral degree from Kyushu University, with specializations available in Engineering, Science, or Interdisciplinary Studies.

Toki City, Gifu Prefecture, where our laboratory is located, has flourished as a traditional pottery town since the Sengoku period along with the neighboring Tajimi City. In Toki, traditional craftsmanship coexists beautifully with cutting-edge plasma research aiming to develop future energy sources. If you are considering graduate studies and would like to visit NIFS, financial support for travel expenses may be available, so please feel free to contact us.

In our laboratory, we conduct plasma confinement research using the Large Helical Device (LHD), a high-temperature plasma confinement device designed for magnetic confinement nuclear fusion. Our core philosophy is to build novel diagnostic devices utilizing lasers and microwaves, and to uncover new physics through their deployment. We engage in our research while maintaining a strong awareness of both its pure physical interest and how our findings will contribute to the design of future commercial fusion reactors. (LHD plasma experiments concluded in December 2025. Our future research is transitioning to the analysis of the vast amounts of historical LHD experimental data, the CHD project scheduled to start operation in 2027, and experimental collaboration on the large-scale device JT-60SA in Naka City, Ibaraki Prefecture.)

The Great East Japan Earthquake on March 11, 2011, and the subsequent Fukushima nuclear power plant accident provided profound lessons to the world. In fact, the electrical energy textbooks I studied as a university student in the late 1980s stated that the probability of a severe nuclear reactor accident was lower than that of a meteorite striking the Earth. However, the Chernobyl accident in the former Soviet Union (now Ukraine) in 1986 (preceded by the Three Mile Island accident in the US in 1979), and the Fukushima accident in 2011 completely shattered the myth of absolute safety surrounding nuclear fission power. While one can criticize past safety management from a third-party perspective, as researchers dedicated to developing energy sources based on nuclear reactions—even though our fusion methodology is fundamentally different and inherently safer—we must treat these historical events as our own concern and continue to learn from them. Before diving into the specific research details of our laboratory, we will first introduce the principles of nuclear fission power generation currently supplying electricity, alongside the future nuclear fusion power generation we aim to achieve.
